About Steve Gottlieb

Steve Gottlieb is one of the most prolific visual deep sky observers in the history of amateur astronomy. His decades-long systematic survey of the NGC, IC, and UGC catalogs represents an unparalleled personal record of the deep sky.

Born in San Francisco, Gottlieb began serious deep sky observing in 1978, starting with the Messier objects through a 6-inch reflector. What began as a personal exploration of the night sky evolved into a monumental 35-year project: visually observing every object in the New General Catalogue accessible from the northern hemisphere.

In 2014, he completed all NGC objects north of −41° declination from his observing sites in northern California. Three years later, after multiple trips to Australia for the far southern sky, he observed his final NGC object — NGC 2932 — on October 18, 2017 at the OzSky Star Safari in New South Wales.

Following the NGC, Gottlieb turned his attention to the Index Catalogue (IC), completing its nearly 1,529 galaxy entries. The last IC object, IC 1493, was observed on October 18, 2025 — exactly eight years to the day after finishing the NGC.

Telescopes and Observing

Throughout his career, Gottlieb has used progressively larger instruments:

  • 6" f/5 reflector — Early Messier work (1978–1980)
  • 13.1" Odyssey I — First NGC explorations (1981–1986)
  • 17.5" f/4.5 homemade Dobsonian — Primary instrument (1987–2002)
  • 18" f/4.3 Starmaster — Extended surveys (2003–2011)
  • 24" f/3.7 Starstructure — Current primary telescope (2012–present)
  • 48" Dobsonian — Over 500 observations using Jimi Lowrey's giant telescope in west Texas

Publications and Contributions

Gottlieb has authored 46 articles for Sky & Telescope and Astronomy magazines, covering topics from galaxy clusters to planetary nebulae, interacting galaxies to Wolf-Rayet bubbles. He is also a contributor to the NGC/IC Project, helping verify historical identifications of thousands of deep sky objects.

His work has been recognized with the Astronomical League's Herschel 400 award and he has been a featured speaker at numerous star parties and astronomy conventions across the United States and Australia.
